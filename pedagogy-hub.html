  • Problem-Based Learning (PBL) is a pedagogical approach where students learn through solving a complex real-world problem that has no single correct answer. Students work collaboratively to identify what they need to learn, engage in self-directed learning, apply that new knowledge to the problem, and then reflect on both what they learned and how effective their strategies were. The teacher acts as a facilitator rather than a knowledge provider. The various stages of PBL also provide opportunities to develop the Ways of Thinking and Doing (WOTD) in students.
Key Question:

How should Singapore manage the impact of acidic rainfall on infrastructure and heritage building conservation?

Singapore's heritage buildings are more than old structures; they are tangible records of the nation's history, cultural identity and collective memory. Landmarks such as churches, monuments, memorials, and colonial-era buildings connect present-day Singaporeans to their past. Once damaged, many of their original features and architectural details cannot be fully restored.

In this Problem-Based Learning experience, students explore the challenge of protecting heritage buildings from the long-term effects of acidic rainfall. Although Singapore does not experience acid rain at the same severity as some heavily industrialised regions, air pollutants can dissolve in rainwater to form acids. Over time, acidic rainfall can react with building materials such as limestone and marble, which contain calcium carbonate. These reactions gradually erode surfaces, weaken structures, and remove intricate carvings and inscriptions that hold historical significance.

Using heritage conservation as the context helps students develop an affective connection to the issue. Rather than learning acid-base chemistry as an abstract topic, students engage with a real-world problem that has local relevance and social significance. They consider how scientific knowledge can contribute to preserving places that matter to Singapore and future generations.

The context also presents a hopeful and action-oriented perspective. While the effects of acidic rainfall can accumulate over long periods, scientific understanding enables early detection, preventive maintenance, material selection, and conservation strategies that can reduce damage. Students investigate the chemistry behind acidic rainfall, analyse evidence on its impact on different materials, and evaluate possible mitigation measures.

  • The learning experience described above exemplifiesopen inquiry, where students exercise a high degree of ownership over the inquiry process. Inquiry-based learning (IBL) can be differentiated according to students' readiness by varying the extent of teacher guidance and student self-direction across the essential features of inquiry.
  • Regardless of the level of guidance provided, the core intent of IBL remains unchanged.Students engage with authentic questions, gather and use evidence to construct explanations, persevere through uncertainty, and develop curiosity about the natural world.
  • The degree of "openness" in an inquiry is determined by the extent to which students assume responsibility for posing questions, collecting and analysing evidence, constructing and evaluating explanations, and communicating their findings.
  • To develop students' inquiry capabilities progressively, learning experiences should vary in their level of openness. Across their science learning journey, students should have opportunities to engage in a range of inquiry experiences--from confirmation and structured inquiry to guided and open inquiry--as they build the knowledge, skills, and dispositions needed to inquire independently.

  • Socio-Scientific Issue-based Learning (SSI-L) is a pedagogical approach where students learn science through engaging withauthentic,open-endedandsocially relevant issuesthat are closely connected to scientific knowledge. These issues often involve ethical, social,economic, environmental or political considerations, and usually do not have a single correct answer.www.channelnewsasia.com
  • SSI-L is especially useful when the issue:
  • is authentic and meaningful to students
  • is connected to scientific concepts,evidenceand practices
  • involves ethical,societalor environmentalimplications
  • requires students to consider multiple perspectivesand trade-offs
  • allows for more than one defensible position
  • supports students in making informed decisions and taking responsible action
  • SSI-L helps students move beyond learning science as a body of knowledge. It enables them to apply scientific understanding to real-world issues, evaluate evidence, reason through trade-offs, and justify decisions using both scientific and ethical considerations.
  • A typical SSI-L learning experience involves three connected phases:
  • Encountering aFocalIssue
  • Students encounter a meaningful socio-scientific issue and develop awareness of its scientific and societal dimensions.
  • Engaging with keyIdeas andPractices
  • Students develop and apply Disciplinary Core Ideas, Scientific Practices and Crosscutting Concepts while engaging in socio-scientific reasoning. This includes considering evidence, stakeholders, perspectives, values, uncertainty and the limitations of science.
  • SynthesisingKeyIdeas andPractices
  • Students draw together their scientific understanding and reasoning to develop, communicate and justify a position, recommendation, policy, solution or course of action.
  • Across the learning sequence, students should also have opportunities to evaluate the reliability of informationsources andreflect on or refine their own beliefs and positions regarding the issue.
  • The following questions are characteristic of SSI-L:
  • What is the socio-scientific issue we are examining?
  • What scientific knowledge is needed to understand the issue?
  • What evidence is available, and how reliable is it?
  • Who are the stakeholders affected by this issue?
  • What ethical principles or values are involved?
  • What different perspectives or trade-offs need to be considered?
  • What decision or course of action can be justified using science, evidence and ethical reasoning?
  • In the context of science education, SSI-L supports students in developing scientific literacy by helping them understand how science interacts with society. It also provides opportunities to develop the Practices of Science, especially in using evidence, evaluating claims, constructing arguments and making informed decisions.
  • This LE is situatedwithin a lesson which includes allcomponents of the SSI-L framework. (Note: Not all lessons need to have allcomponents)
  • SSI Ethical Dilemma: Should vaccination against Disease X be compulsory in Singapore?
  • Disease X is a novel disease. It is highly contagious and can lead to serious respiratory problems or even death, especially in people who have weaker immune systems. Many patients would need to be hospitalised as they will require respiratory support. The vaccine for Disease X has been recently developed and can be used for ages 12 years and above.
  • You will now take on the roles of lawmakers in Singapore and imagine that you are in the situation where Singapore has many Disease X infections. The vaccine against Disease X has just been developed and approved for use by the World Health Organization.
